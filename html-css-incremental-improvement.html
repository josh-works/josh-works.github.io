A Little CSS Goes a Long Way

by Josh Thompson

heavily inspired by (or really copied from) Web Design in 4 Min (jgthms.com)

start over again

I wanted to learn some basic styling. I've done back-end development most of my professional career, hardly ever had to weigh in on the front-end.

But, over here on my own personal website, I've got a bunch of modifications I want to make.

Specifically, I want come up with a way to add footnotes and annotations to my own writing. Maybe something similar to how Nate Berkopec (example) or Tim Urban (example) does it.

So, I'm here to talk about styling, html, css, and javascript, but what is more important than all of those things?

Words! Feelings! Information!

Certainly many websites deliver things besides words (Netflix, Youtube) but I'd argue most of stuff we do on the web comes down to reading text.

For example, you're reading this text right now

These words are sometimes called "content", but I would argue that labeling this as simple 'content' undervalues what is happening here.

See, people are memetic

Without going too far into this, I'm broadly classifying this as "people are strongly influenced by ideas and beliefs, and ideas and beliefs can spread through a person's mind and a group of people just like a virus might."

But the best words, the most compelling ideas, if presented in an unreadable way, will fail to have an effect.

as a proof of concept, when is the last time you've found an idea, written in Thai, to shape your daily actions?

Probably never, unless you happen to be a Thai speaker.

Parenthetically, it causes me discomfort to think of all amazing ideas and stories in the world, that are amazing precicely because the author/story-teller has no knowledge of English.

I will never know these ideas, because we cannot converse directly with each other.

Anyway, we ought to care about legibility.

To that end, lets improve the styling here a little bit.

in our styles.css document:

    body {
      margin: 0 auto;
      max-width: 50em;
    }
